The Pulse: ‘Tokenmaxxing’ as a weird new trend

Gergely Orosz documents how internal token leaderboards at Meta, Microsoft, and Salesforce turned AI adoption into a gamified spend metric, pushing engineers toward wasteful prompting, throwaway agent runs, and avoidable quality failures. The strongest operational lesson is Shopify’s narrower model: measure usage, cut off runaway spend, and inspect expensive sessions without rewarding raw token volume.

  • Token totals become a gamed proxy metric as soon as status, review pressure, or quotas are attached to them.
  • Leaderboard-driven AI adoption can reduce quality by rewarding volume over outcome and by normalizing unnecessary agent runs.
  • Circuit breakers and review of unusual spend are safer controls than public rankings of who burned the most tokens.
  • Internal overuse incentives may also be serving as an expensive way to generate real-world traces for model training.

Recreating Apple’s Vision Pro Animation in CSS

John Rhea rebuilds Apple’s Vision Pro scroll animation with modern CSS primitives, using view timelines, sticky positioning, grid-overlaid background layers, and viewport-derived geometry to reproduce a complex staged effect without JavaScript scroll control. The useful signal is in the implementation tradeoffs: responsive motion becomes tractable, while Firefox support and frame-by-frame image swapping remain hard limits.

  • A single CSS grid cell with layered background elements can recreate the front-and-back depth effect without absolute-position sprawl.
  • View timelines plus a sticky container let the animation begin when the component enters view and stay pinned while it plays.
  • Custom properties derived from viewport dimensions make multi-stage translation distances responsive enough for intricate scroll animation.
  • Replacing scroll-scrubbed video with keyframed background-image swaps works as a proof of concept and increases asset and browser-support costs.

Pluralistic: The (other) problem with automatic conversion of free software to proprietary software (23 Apr 2026)

Cory Doctorow argues that AI clean-room clones of free software do create pressure on the commons, and that the headline proprietary-licensing threat is overstated because fully AI-generated reimplementations are uncopyrightable and land in the public domain. He places the more immediate enclosure mechanisms elsewhere: SaaS loopholes, tivoization, and the ways companies already capture free software without reciprocating.

  • Clean-room reimplementation separates function from expression, so reproducing behavior is distinct from copying protected source code.
  • A company cannot rely on fully AI-generated replacement code as proprietary inventory if that code lacks copyright protection.
  • Server-side deployment and hardware lockdown already weaken copyleft more effectively than speculative AI cloning schemes.
  • Reimplementation is part of free-software history, so the strategic question is how licensing and governance adapt to new tooling.

TBM 417: Before You Fire All Your Glue People Because of AI

John Cutler examines how organizations misapply AI heuristics to invisible coordination work and end up stripping out the people who preserve context, follow-through, and cross-team coherence. It is worth keeping because it gives a durable test for deciding which work AI can accelerate and which work still depends on human glue.

  • Visible artifacts are a weak proxy for the coordination work underneath them; replacing the person while keeping the artifact can hollow out the system.
  • AI is strongest on work that teams already agree should happen and can describe clearly; ambiguous cross-functional maintenance needs a higher skepticism level.
  • Staff reductions driven by surface-level heuristics can create delayed structural failures that only appear after the coordinating people are gone.
  • Evaluate candidate AI substitutions by tracing who currently creates context, drives follow-through, and resolves edge-case ambiguity.

Reality check around requirements management

This r/ExperiencedDevs thread describes a long-lived enterprise system whose requirements were reverse-engineered into a structured repository, then allowed to decay back into scattered files, oral history, and dead tooling. It is worth keeping because it captures the operational cost of treating system behavior knowledge as optional documentation instead of core infrastructure.

  • Requirements knowledge decays quickly when the steady-state editing workflow is weaker than the original transformation workflow.
  • Deleting old trackers and document stores before migration completes destroys the recovery paths teams rely on for edge-case behavior.
  • Large domain-heavy systems accumulate month-long investigation cycles when behavior, rationale, and test intent are scattered across people and files.
  • Living specifications are a delivery asset for support, onboarding, testing, and safe change management.

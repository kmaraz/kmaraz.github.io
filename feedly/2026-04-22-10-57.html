TBM 418: Campfires, Trails, and Quests

John Cutler argues that AI improves product work when it enriches the team’s loop of traces and conversations, and worsens it when it mass-produces polished artifacts that preserve broken planning models. The useful pattern is multiplayer: pair prompting, shared context pointers, and AI-assisted synthesis that compound into better conversations and fresher mental models.

  • AI amplifies broken product rituals if it is used to mass-produce requirements, plans, and status artifacts without changing how teams learn.
  • Shared context pointers, pair prompting, and evolving traces make AI valuable because they raise the leverage of cross-functional conversations.
  • The core output of good AI collaboration is shared understanding that carries into later decisions, not a standalone deliverable.
  • Teams need loops where trails feed campfires and campfires update trails so the context graph keeps improving over time.

The Physiology of Agency in the Age of AI

Deric Bownds argues that agency is a physiological, load-bearing emotion, so AI systems that shift people from initiating and judging work to merely ratifying machine suggestions could create learned-helplessness-like costs long before society notices them. The essay is strongest when it reframes AI agency loss as a design and public-health question rather than a free-will debate.

  • Wegner’s work on conscious will and Seligman’s learned helplessness research suggest perceived agency affects motivation, regulation, and health.
  • AI can preserve agency when the human remains the initiator, director, and final judge of the collaboration.
  • Systems that pre-structure reflection and decision-making too aggressively risk pushing users into passive approval loops.
  • Interface and workflow design should protect authorship and self-regulation, not only convenience and speed.

incident.io documents a concrete performance rescue for alert-history filtering: the team combined a custom bloom-filter pass over JSONB attributes with a mandatory 30-day time bound and cut P95 latency for large organizations from 5s to 0.3s. The post is valuable because it shows the full tradeoff process, including why the data model, pagination scheme, and product default had to change together.

  • Sparse, customer-defined JSONB attributes made filtering expensive even before query plans became pathological at scale.
  • A bloom-filter precheck reduced the number of rows that needed expensive in-memory attribute evaluation.
  • A mandatory 30-day default window fixed the longer-term scaling problem by aligning the query with actual user recency bias.
  • The team chose the more complex bloom-filter path because it looked less likely to create a second round of index-bloat problems later.

Everyone in the company is an engineer now. Any chance of containing this?

An r/ExperiencedDevs thread on non-engineers shipping production-facing vibe-coded apps captures a governance failure that is likely to spread: organizations expanding AI-assisted prototyping into production mutation without requiring system understanding, ownership, or security authority. The thread is worth keeping because it names the real burden shift: engineers inherit the maintenance, privacy, and incident risk from workflows they do not control.

  • Giving non-technical staff production access creates hidden operational debt even when the generated apps look useful.

  • Teams need explicit boundaries between prototyping and production changes, with development and security able to block unsafe rollout.

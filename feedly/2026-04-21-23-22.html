HTTP resilience tradeoffs in practice: retry vs Retry-After vs hedging (scenario data)

This post compares three failure-handling tactics under controlled network chaos and surfaces the operational tradeoffs clearly: naive retries can raise success while worsening tail latency, Retry-After-aware retry helps in rate-limit scenarios, and hedging lowers latency by spending extra capacity.

  • Retry policy needs latency budgets and load budgets, because higher success rates can still degrade p95 and p99 behavior.
  • Honoring Retry-After is a distinct control path from generic backoff and matters specifically in 429-heavy environments.
  • Hedging is a tail-latency tool with real duplicate-work cost, so it belongs on carefully chosen endpoints.

Frederick Vanbrabant argues that architecture governance works best when approved building blocks become the default path of least resistance, moving teams away from architecture boards and embedded oversight toward paved-road standards that already package security, logging, and legal requirements.
